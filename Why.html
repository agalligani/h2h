Along a 26 mile long thread of L.A. tarmac today, thousands of dedicated souls are pushing their
minds against their rejecting bodies, countering the heat and exhaustion with a will to make it to
the finish line and bask in the achievement of having withstood the tortures of the 22nd Los
Angeles Marathon. It seems fitting then that on this very day, the 4th of March 2007, Wendy and I
should finally commit to staging our own private 25,000 mile marathon around the globe. Ever
since the completion of our trip from Los Angeles to Istanbul seven years ago, this life objective of
riding a motorcycle around the world has lurked there in the shadows and I have never been
able to conclude whether it is friend or foe. A task of this magnitude equally has the ability to
remold you, to recast what you believe can be done and to exponentially leverage an already
abundant world view, but also to bite you hard in any one of a number of ways, the worst of
which life threatening. But it will not go away, as Everest never will, and my mind will not rest until
the deed is done. For those who followed our pages to Istanbul in 2000, the reason for the doing
this trip, in this way have not changed. Both Wendy and I have traveled extensively, me almost
incessantly, for many years now. But where all those hundreds of thousands of miles were laid
behind me as I sat in a giant, aluminum cigar tube punching a worm hole through the
stratosphere, bugger all oblivious to the love, hate, beauty, ugliness, life and death going on
40,000 feet below, carving a sinuous ribbon of tire track on the ground, on a motorcycle, exposed
to all for better or for worse, will give us the chance to make up for everything we have missed.
The world is not just bars, restaurants and clubs in New York, London, Cape Town and Manila
(those who know me are choking on their own spit right now), it is also all the things in between: it
is the fishing villagers in Thailand who give you their beer and sing karaoke with you; it is the old
lady in the desolate village in Baja who offers to repair your knackered boot with mop string, free
of charge; and it is the airport worker in Cambodia who befriends you and sniggers as you gag
on your first gulp of cobra wine as he recounts his own personal tale of the Killing Fields. It is at this
human level, at ground level, that we want to make our own new discoveries.
